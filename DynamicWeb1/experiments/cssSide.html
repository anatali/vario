Introduction

  • Quantum circuits are a model for quantum computation in which a computation is a sequence of quantum gates followed by measurements.
  • The gates are reversible transformations on a quantum mechanical analog of an n-bit register. This analogous structure is referred to as an n-qubit register.
  • Measurements, drawn on a circuit diagram as stylized pointer dials, indicate where and how a result is obtained from the quantum computer after the steps of the computation are executed.
  • Without loss of generality, one can work with the standard circuit model, in which the set of gates are single-qubit unitary transformations and controlled NOT gates on pairs of qubits, and all measurements are in the computational basis.
  • Measurement-based quantum computation (MBQC) is a model of quantum computing in which the answer to a question is, informally speaking, created in the act of measuring the physical system that serves as the computer.

Measurement

In quantum mechanics, each physical system is associated with a Hilbert space, each element of which is a wave function that represents a possible state of the physical system. The approach codified by John von Neumann represents measurement upon a physical system by a self-adjoint operator (Hermitian operator) \( O \) on that Hilbert space termed an "observable". Thus \( O = O^†\).
The eigenvalues of an Hermitian operator are real numbers and each possible outcome of a measurement is an eigenvalue of the observable.

About phases (si veda anche Blochdemo)

  • Arbitrary quantum state: \( \ket{ψ} = α\ket{0} + β\ket{1} \) with α,β complex numbers.
  • Born rule: result 0 has probability \( |α|^2 \), result 1 has probability \( |β|^2 \) and \( |α|^2 + |β|^2=1 \).
  • Global phase (see Quantum phase) of a quantum state not detectable (i.e. \( {\color{blue}\ket{ψ} = e^{iγ}\ket{ψ}} \text{ con } γ ∈ [0,2π] \) ) [remember also the Euler equation: \( e^{iγ} = cos(γ)+i\text{ }sin(γ) \) and that \( e^{iπ} = -1 \) ]: $$ \ket{ψ} = α\ket{0} + β\ket{1} = {\color{blue}\sqrt{1-p} \ket{0} + e^{iφ}\sqrt{p}\ket{1}} $$ where \( 0 ≤ p ≤ 1\) is the probability of the bit being in the 1 state, and \( 0 ≤ φ < 2π \) is the quantum phase.
    Now we can write: $$ \ket{ψ} = {\color{fuchsia}a\ket{0} + e^{iφ}b\ket{1} \text{ con } a,b ∈ R} $$ but, since \( \sqrt{ \cos^2{x} + \sin^2{x} } = 1 \) we can describe the real a and b in terms of one variable θ: \( a=\cos{θ} \text{ , } b=\sin{θ} \), so that the most general state of a single qubit can take the form:
    \( \ket{ψ} = {\color{blue} \cos{\frac{θ}{2}}}\ket{0} + {\color{blue} e^{iφ}sin{\frac{θ}{2}}}\ket{1} \color{fuchsia}\text{ with } θ,φ ∈ R \)
    This leads to representation of the Bloch sphere.
